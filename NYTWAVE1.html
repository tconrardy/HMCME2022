Ukraine Pursues NATO Membership Following Confrontation with Russia

Following a skirmish on the border between Russia and Ukraine and an uncoordinated joint response from Ukraine and NATO, the military bloc is initiating accession talks with Ukraine.

Russian tanks briefly crossed into Ukraine before returning to Russian soil on Monday.

By Kat McKatington

Jan. 20, 2022

KYIEV — On Monday, the international community floundered in the face of the crisis that the West had been dreading: a Russian attack on the Ukranian border. Ukranian military personel stationed along Ukraine's eastern border alerted Ukranian and NATO forces Monday morning that at least 12 Russian tanks had breached the Ukranian border. NATO leaders mobilized troops to meet the threat, but not before Ukraine sent sent 3,000 of its own troops to meet the Russian intruders. Ukraine then called its troops back to coordinate with NATO forces, by which point Russia had already pulled its forces back beyond the border. Russia now claims that the entire situation was a training mishap. Ukranian and NATO military leaders warn that it was a practice invasion.

Monday's events only furter exposed inefficiencies in the working relationship between Ukraine and regional military block NATO. Following weeks of troop movements, Russia now has over 110,000 troops stationed on the Ukranian border. Military analysts have raised questions as to how well NATO and Ukranian forces would be able to work together in the face of a full-scale invasion, especially given clunky working protocols between the two formally unaffiliated parties. Ukrainian president Volodymyr Zelensky has raised similar concerns, and publicly called yesterday for the opening of accession talks with NATO.

The opening of accession proceedings would be a dramatic shift in NATO policy. While the bloc has formally claimed that it is open to further expansion (and inducted North Macedonia in 2020,) Russian president Vladimir Putin has expressed that NATO expansion will be met with animosity. Induction of Ukraine—Russia's immediate neighbor and seeming military target—would almost certainly further aggravate Russia and further inflame tensions with the West.

US Secretary of State Antony Blinken noted that while the US still seeks peaceful resolution with Russia, that induction of Ukraine into NATO may increase military readiness and send a powerful message to the Russian government. "We could send NATO troops to the border and engage in the same kind of dangerous brinkmanship that we've seen from Russia in recent weeks," Blinken said in a press counference yesterday. "Or we can consider more measured action, like the accession talks proposed by president Zelensky. State Department diplomats will be working closely with our colleagues at the Pentagon to ensure that further steps best protect the livelihoods of the Ukranian people, the values of the American people, and the strategic interests of NATO."

Given similar statements of support from a number of other NATO member-states, it seems likely that accession talks may unfold in the days to come. President Putin has remained silent on the issue, but Russian minister of foreign affairs Sergey Lavrov addressed the prospect of Ukranian NATO accession in a press conference during his visit to Beijing earlier today. "Russia has no malicious intentions, but in the face of an ever-expanding military coalition organized around the principle of attacking our nation, what are Russians expected to do? NATO is an ardently anti-Russian military collective, and the threat of NATO expansion will be addressed accordingly."
