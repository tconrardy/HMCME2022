In Wake of Marea and Dunant Cable Outages, Russia Denies Responsibility, Hints at Further Escalation

Russian president Vladimir Putin has denied Russian submarines cut two major underwater cables ealier this week, but Russian officials implied that further cable outages will ensue if NATO accession talks proceed.

Putin has remained silent in the face of claims from multiple intelligence agency's assertions that Russian submarines sabatoged cables that handle millions of users' internet traffic.

By Meowry Mousington

Jan. 22, 2022

Ukraine's planned accesion to NATO was thrown into freefall last week as world leaders scrambled to explain sudden stalls in global internet traffic. It was established later that day that both the Marea and Dunant cables had been disrupted, reportedly having been cut by Russian submarines.

Ukraine first requested to initiate NATO accesion talks after Russia sent 12 tanks into Ukrainian territory last week. NATO agreed, and member states were scheduled to sign off on Ukranian accession protocols late last week. This meeting was cancelled as Europe and the US scrambled to resolve major internet outages which intelligence eventually found were due to disruptions of the Marea and Dunant cables, reportedly caused by Russian submarines.

Russia's submarine fleet has been slowly increasing in size over the course of the last decade. National security experts warn that while tactical submarines can be used for espionage and underwater sabatoge like the disruption of the Marant and Durea cables, that attack submarines also constitute a significant portion of the Russian fleet.

Putin has seemingly taken a vested interest in Russia's submarine fleet in recent years, taking marked steps to build new ships capable of harboring submarines and to rehaul Cold-War-era nuclear submarines to serve new tactical purposes. The Russian government claims that most of these new submarines were designed for marine research, but Russia-watchers have warned that many of the new submarines have military capabilities.

As the situation stands, it seems unlikely that Russia will launch a full-fledged attack on the global network of underwater internet cables, (to cut the US off from foreign internet traffic, Russia would have to take out at least 18 seperate cables,) but experts warn that the advanced nature of Russia's submarine fleet puts Russia in a strong position to incrementally sever more cables if negotiations with Western powers aren't favoring the Kremlin. Russian politics expert and Harvard University professor Jimothy Colton noted that such a strategy would fall squarely in line with line with Russia's broader strategy of "hybrid warfare."

"Russia has the fifth-largest military in the world, but doesn't have the standing military might to challenge NATO or even just the US," Colton said. "Russia has, however, developed a number of less conventional methods of pressuring foreign opponents. We've seen Russian interference in a number of democratic elections, which is hugely destabilizing for Western leadership. We've seen misinformation campaigns. We've seen intelligence efforts that far exceed the sophistication of Russian traditional military activity. Russia isn't cutting cables because slowing down American and European internet will change the tides of a military dispute; Russia is cutting cables one by one to exert pressure for NATO to back down without ever firing an actual shot."

Despite Russia's seeming plans to use underwater cable sabatoge as leverage during talks with NATO, the Kremlin has remained reserved about its capabilities and intentions. Russian President Vladimir Putin has yet to publicly address allegations of Russian involvement in the cable outages, though Russian Defense Minister Sergey Shoygu cryptically alluded to the allegations during a press conference with the Chinese Defense Secretary in Beijing.

"Russia has no knowledge of, nor involvement in, the reported outages of the Dunant or Marea cables in the Atlantic Ocean," said Shoygu. "With that said, I think that this crisis only further demonstrates that NATO should reconsider its actions before proceeding with any hasty decisions pertaining to dangerous and aggressive expansion of their military bloc."

NATO has yet to reschedule the signing of Ukraine's accession protocols, though several European leaders, including President Zelensky of Ukraine and French President Emmanuel Macron, have publicly advocated to resume accession proceedings.
